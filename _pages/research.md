My research examines how climate variability, environmental change, and extreme weather shape infectious disease risk and population health. I use epidemiologic, spatial, and forecasting methods to understand when and where climate-sensitive health risks emerge, intensify, and shift over time.

## Current Research

::: {.research-grid}

::: {.research-card}
### Climate, Flooding, and Child Health in India

As a postdoctoral scholar at Stanford University, I study how flooding and other hydrologic extremes affect child health and mortality in India. This work links high-resolution flood exposure data with population-based health datasets, including DHS/NFHS birth histories and mortality data.

The goal is to estimate the acute and delayed health impacts of flooding during pregnancy and early childhood, with a focus on vulnerable populations and public health decision-making.

:::

::: {.research-card}
### Flood Exposure and Mortality Using the Million Death Study

I am working to link flood exposure estimates with mortality outcomes from the Million Death Study. This project examines whether flooding is associated with short-term increases in all-cause and cause-specific mortality.

By integrating verbal autopsy data, geospatial linkage, and time-varying environmental exposures, this work aims to improve understanding of the mortality burden attributable to flooding in India.

:::

:::

## Valley Fever and Climate-Sensitive Infectious Disease

::: {.research-grid}

::: {.research-card}
### Environmental Epidemiology of Valley Fever

My doctoral research focused on the environmental epidemiology of coccidioidomycosis, or Valley fever, in California. This work examined how climate and environmental conditions influence seasonal transmission, acute risk following extreme weather events, near-term disease forecasts, and long-term climate change projections.

:::

::: {.research-card}
### Climate Change and the Future Distribution of Valley Fever

I use downscaled climate projections and epidemiologic models to estimate how climate change may alter the future burden and geographic distribution of Valley fever in California.

This work evaluates how warming and changing precipitation patterns could shift disease risk across regions, populations, and time periods.

:::

::: {.research-card}
### Extreme Weather and Short-Term Valley Fever Risk

I study how acute environmental extremes, including heat, precipitation, drought, and dust-related conditions, affect short-term Valley fever risk.

These analyses use self-matched epidemiologic designs to clarify the timing and direction of associations between environmental extremes and fungal disease transmission.

:::

::: {.research-card}
### Pediatric Coccidioidomycosis in California

In collaboration with public health partners, I am examining recent increases in pediatric coccidioidomycosis in California.

This project evaluates whether pediatric cases increased unusually in 2023–2024, which counties or regions experienced the largest changes, and whether recent increases are consistent with historical post-drought patterns.

:::

:::

## Forecasting and Public Health Decision Support

::: {.research-grid}

::: {.research-card}
### CocciCast: Near-Term Forecasting of Valley Fever

I developed and maintain a near-term forecasting framework for coccidioidomycosis in California. This work combines historical surveillance data, environmental predictors, autoregressive features, and machine learning models to forecast future Valley fever incidence.

The goal is to produce forecasts that are rigorous, reproducible, and useful for public health decision-making.

:::

::: {.research-card}
### Climate-Sensitive Disease Surveillance

More broadly, I am interested in developing surveillance and modeling tools that help public health agencies detect, anticipate, and respond to climate-sensitive infectious disease risks.

This includes work on environmental forecasting, spatial risk mapping, and methods for translating climate-health research into actionable public health information.

:::

:::
